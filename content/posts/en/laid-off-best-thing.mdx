# I Got Laid Off. And It Might Be the Best Thing That Ever Happened to Me

**A few months ago, I got laid off.**

Sounds like the start of a bad story, right? But let me tell you—sometimes the "bad news" life hands you is actually a door to something new.

## That Day Was Just Like Any Other

I woke up, opened my laptop, and started working. As a backend developer, my days were pretty much the same: write code, tweak requirements, fix bugs, then start the whole cycle again.

I'd been living this way for years. Two points, one line. Office and home.

**A lot of people think being a programmer is a pretty good gig.** The pay's decent, and it's technically challenging.

But anyone who's been in this industry long enough knows the truth: it gets repetitive. Every day, it's the same requirements, the same code, the same projects. Everyone on the team is exhausted. Overtime isn't the exception anymore—it's the default.

After a while, you start to notice something: **most people have lost that spark they once had. Including me.**

That enthusiasm from when I first started—the drive to build something, to change something—slowly got ground down by the day-to-day grind.

Sometimes I'd catch myself wondering: **If I leave this company tomorrow, what skills do I actually have left?**

Honestly? I didn't have an answer.

## The Layoff Finally Came

And then, one day, it happened. The company announced layoffs.

When I got the news, I wasn't devastated. I wasn't even surprised. What I felt was something far more practical: **life goes on.**

I didn't have crushing debt or financial pressure, but the future still felt uncertain. The next step was obvious: update the resume, send out applications, wait for replies. Every programmer knows this drill.

## But This Time, Something Felt Different

Over the past year, AI has been moving fast. Really fast. Things that used to take developers hours can now be done with AI assistance.

At the same time, the entire tech industry was cooling down. More layoff announcements. Fewer job openings.

We used to think programming was a stable career with endless demand. Now? Nobody seems so sure anymore.

**Maybe this industry is entering a whole new phase.**

I don't know if that's true. But I do know I'm not as certain as I used to be.

## In My Anxiety, I Stumbled Upon a Community

During that period, I spent a lot of time browsing tech forums. One day, I came across a group I'd never really paid attention to before: **indie developers.**

They're different from traditional programmers. They don't write code for a company—they build their own products, launch them, and run them themselves.

Some build small tools. Some build SaaS products. Some make browser extensions. Many of them work alone.

But they're all doing something interesting: **working for themselves.**

## That's When It Hit Me

For years, I'd been writing code. But almost all of it was for someone else.

The company's product. The company's business. The company's goals.

If the company no longer needed those things tomorrow, everything I'd built would have nothing to do with me anymore.

That was the first time I seriously asked myself: **What if I started building something for myself?**

## This Path Isn't Easy

I know becoming an indie developer isn't simple.

The biggest challenge? **Results take forever.** Some products need months or years before you see any traction. Some might never succeed at all.

No steady paycheck. No clear roadmap. No one telling you what to do next.

But even knowing all that, this idea gave me something I hadn't felt in a long time.

**Excitement.**

## Now Is Actually the Best Time

I thought about it for a long time.

If I stayed on the traditional path, I'd probably get another job, do more of the same work, and maybe face another layoff a few years down the road. I'd be older, the competition would be fiercer, and making a change would be even harder.

So why not try something different **now**?

## Starting from Zero

Right now, I don't have much.

No finished product. No users. No steady income.

Just one idea: **I'm going to try becoming an indie developer.**

Maybe the road will be long. Maybe I'll fail a bunch of times.

But at least this is a path I'm choosing for myself.

## One Day, I'll Look Back

Maybe someday I'll look back on today.

And I might realize that getting laid off wasn't actually a bad thing. It just forced me to confront a question I'd been avoiding: **What kind of life do I actually want?**

Today is day one of my new beginning.

**Let's go. Future me, I hope you don't regret this.**
