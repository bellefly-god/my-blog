# The Day I Got Laid Off Was the Best Thing That Happened to Me

A few months ago, I got laid off.

It sounds like the beginning of a sad story. But let me tell you something—sometimes the "bad news" life throws at you is actually a door to somewhere new.

---

## It started like any other day

I woke up, opened my laptop, and started working. Just like every other day.

As a backend developer, my routine was pretty set: write code, deal with requirements, fix bugs, then do it all over again. Years of this. Every single day.

**Home and office. That was it.**

People outside the industry probably think being a programmer is pretty good. Decent pay, technical work, the whole deal. But if you've been doing this for a while, you know the truth—it's repetitive. Boring, even.

Same requirements. Same code. Same projects. Everyone on the team is exhausted. Overtime became the default, not the exception.

After enough time, you start to notice something:

> *Most people lost their spark a long time ago. Me included.*

That excitement you had when you first started—when you actually wanted to build things and make an impact—it fades. Slowly. Day by day.

Sometimes I'd ask myself: **If I left this company tomorrow, what would actually set me apart?**

Honestly? I didn't have an answer.

---

## Then the layoffs came

Eventually, it happened. The company announced cuts.

When I got the news, I wasn't shocked. I was just... resigned. **Life goes on.**

No big debt, no huge pressure. But there's still a long road ahead. So what do you do? Same thing every developer does:

1. Update the resume
2. Send out applications
3. Wait

---

## But this time, something felt different

The past year, AI has been moving fast. Like, really fast. Things that used to take developers hours? AI can handle some of that now.

And the tech industry as a whole? It's cooling down. Layoffs everywhere. Fewer job postings. Less certainty.

Used to be people said programming was stable. High demand, good money. But now? Nobody's saying that anymore.

**Maybe this industry is changing. Maybe it's becoming something different.**

I don't know if that's true. But for the first time, I wasn't so sure about anything.

---

## When I was spiraling, I found these people

During that rough period, I'd scroll through tech communities late at night. And one day, I stumbled across a group I'd never really paid attention to before:

### Indie Makers

They're different from traditional developers. They don't write code for a company. They build their own products, launch them themselves, run everything on their own.

- Some make small tools
- Some build SaaS
- Some create browser extensions
- Many are just one person

**But they're doing something interesting: they're working for themselves.**

---

## And in that moment, something clicked

For years, I've been writing code. But that code? It was always for someone else.

The company's product. The company's goals. The company's roadmap.

If the company decided they didn't need it anymore, what did any of it really mean to me?

That's when I first asked myself seriously:

> **What if I built things for myself?**

---

## It's not an easy path

I know becoming an indie maker isn't simple. Everyone talks about how hard it is.

**The biggest problem? Results take forever.**

Products can take months—or even years—to gain traction. Some might never work out at all.

- No steady paycheck
- No clear roadmap
- Nobody telling you what to do next

But despite all that, something happened. Something I hadn't felt in a long time.

**Excitement.**

---

## If not now, when?

I thought about it for a while.

If I stayed on the same path, I'd probably just switch companies and do the same thing. A few years down the road, maybe the same situation again. Older. More competition. Harder to make a change.

**So why not try something different? Why not now?**

---

## Starting from zero

Right now, I don't have much.

- No finished product
- No users
- No stable income

Just one thing: **I'm going to try being an indie maker.**

Maybe it'll take forever. Maybe I'll fail a bunch of times. But at least, it's my choice.

---

## Someday, when I look back

Maybe one day I'll look back on today.

Maybe I'll realize that getting laid off wasn't bad at all. It just forced me to ask a question I'd been avoiding:

**What kind of life do I actually want?**

And today—today is the first day of figuring that out.

---

*Keep going. Hope future me doesn't regret this decision.*
